Getting Started

2 min read
What You'll Learn
  • What D&D is and why millions play it
  • What you need to start playing
  • Your first steps as a new player

Dungeons & Dragons is a tabletop roleplaying game where you and your friends collaborate to tell an epic story together. One person — the Dungeon Master — describes the world and controls monsters and NPCs. Everyone else controls heroic characters, deciding their actions and rolling dice to determine outcomes. It's collaborative storytelling mixed with strategy and chance.

Your First Decision
Interactive Scenario
You're in a tavern in the town of Millhaven. A hooded figure approaches your table and whispers urgently: "I need adventurers willing to explore the forgotten temple to the north. Treasure awaits, but so do dangers. Will you help?"
You accept eagerly! Your enthusiasm impresses the figure. They reveal themselves as an archaeologist seeking an ancient artifact. You'll meet at the temple entrance at dawn.
A clever approach. The figure explains the temple once held a wizard's tower — a powerful grimoire may remain within. They'll share 30% of the treasure.
A mercenary's heart. They offer 50 gold now, plus 200 more if you return with proof of entering the temple's inner chamber.
Key Takeaway
There's no single "right" answer in D&D. Every choice shapes the story differently, and the best moments come from doing what feels right for your character.

The Core Mechanic: Rolling Dice

4 min read
What You'll Learn
  • How the d20 determines success or failure
  • What Difficulty Class (DC) means

At the heart of D&D is the d20 — a 20-sided die. When your character attempts something uncertain, you roll a d20, add your relevant modifier, and compare against a Difficulty Class (DC). Meet or beat the DC and you succeed.

How Combat Works

6 min read
What You'll Learn
  • How initiative and turn order work
  • The action economy: action, bonus action, movement, reaction
  • How attack rolls and damage work

Combat follows a structured turn order. Everyone rolls initiative, and the highest roll goes first. Each turn you get an action, a bonus action, movement, and a reaction.

Your Turn Resources
Action
Attack, cast a spell, dash, dodge, or disengage
Bonus Action
Many abilities grant this extra action (optional)
Movement
Usually 30 feet, split however you like
Reaction
Once per round, triggered by events

~ Combat begins ~
Goblin 1 rolled initiative: 12
You rolled initiative: 16
Goblin 2 rolled initiative: 8
Goblin 3 rolled initiative: 14
Turn order: You → Goblin 3 → Goblin 1 → Goblin 2
~ Your turn ~
You move 30 feet toward Goblin 1 (movement)

Goblin 1 is wounded but standing!
Step-by-Step Combat Phases
Phase 1 — Initiative
Each creature rolls d20 + DEX modifier. Higher rolls act first. This determines the order for the entire encounter.
Phase 2 — Movement
Move up to your speed (usually 30 ft). You can split movement before and after your action. Climb, swim, or crawl at half speed.
Phase 3 — Action
Take one action: Attack, Cast a Spell, Dash, Disengage, Dodge, Help, Hide, or Ready. Your class determines special options.
Phase 4 — Attack Roll
Roll d20 + attack modifier. If the total meets or exceeds the target's Armor Class (AC), you hit.
Phase 5 — Damage
Roll your weapon's damage dice + modifier. On a natural 20 (critical hit), double the damage dice!
Phase 6 — Bonus & Reaction
Some abilities grant a bonus action on your turn. Reactions trigger on anyone's turn (e.g. attacks of opportunity).
Phase 7 — Next Turn
The next creature in initiative acts. Combat continues until one side surrenders, flees, or falls.
Key Takeaway
Combat is structured but flexible. You always have options — attack, retreat, use the environment, or try something creative. The best tactical moments come from combining movement, actions, and teamwork.

Magic & Spellcasting

4 min read
What You'll Learn
  • How spell slots and cantrips work
  • The eight schools of magic
  • Spell attack rolls vs. saving throws

Spellcasters use spell slots to cast their spells. Each slot is consumed when you cast, and they replenish on a long rest. Cantrips are special — they cost nothing and can be cast indefinitely.

Example — Casting Fireball
A wizard wants to cast Fireball (3rd level). They expend a 3rd-level spell slot, choose a point within 150 feet, and every creature in a 20-foot radius makes a DEX saving throw. Those who fail take 8d6 fire damage. Those who succeed take half.
Spell Save DC Formula
Spell Save DC = 8 + Proficiency Bonus + Spellcasting Modifier

The DM's Role

3 min read
What You'll Learn
  • What the Dungeon Master does during a session
  • Essential tips for running your first game

The Dungeon Master is the storyteller, referee, and world-builder. They describe scenes, control NPCs and monsters, and adjudicate rules. A great DM balances challenge with fun and makes everyone feel heroic.

What a DM Does
🌍
Builds Worlds
Creates settings rich with history and mystery
🎭
Plays NPCs
Voices merchants, kings, and villains
⚔️
Runs Combat
Controls monsters and manages turns
📜
Tells Stories
Weaves player choices into epic narrative
⚖️
Rules Calls
Makes final decisions on ambiguous rules
🎲
Improvises
Adapts when players surprise everyone
Tips for New DMs
Know the basics, improvise the rest. You don't need every rule memorized — your rulings are what matter at the table.
Say "yes, and…" before "no." When players try something creative, find ways to make it work.
Your job is to facilitate fun. The party should feel challenged, not crushed.
Listen to your players. They'll tell you what kind of game they want through their choices.
Mistakes are fine. Everyone makes them. Laugh, move on, and adjust.
